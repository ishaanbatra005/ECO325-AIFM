Money Supply & the Money Multiplier

Understanding how money is created in an economy is the foundation of everything else in this course. There are two core relationships here.

Formula 1 — M3 Growth
M3 growth = Income Elasticity × Real GDP Growth + CPI Inflation
= ε × g + π
Why? M3 is "broad money" — cash + deposits. The RBI sets M3 growth to accommodate real economic growth (scaled by how sensitive money demand is to income = the elasticity) plus inflation. This is the quantity theory of money in action.
WORKED EXAMPLE Q.1 from paper
Given: Real GDP growth = 7%, Inflation = 5%, Income elasticity = 1.2
1

M3 growth = 1.2 × 7% + 5%

2

= 8.4% + 5% = 13.4%

3

Nearest answer = 14.4% (A)... wait — let's re-check: 1.2×7 = 8.4, + 5 = 13.4%. That's closest to B. 13% — Answer is D. None of the above (exact = 13.4%)

✓ ANSWERD — None of the above (13.4%)
Formula 2 — Money Multiplier (α)
α = (1 + c) / (c + r)

c = Currency-Deposit Ratio = Cash held by public / Deposits
r = Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR) = Bank Reserves / Deposits
Intuition: Every rupee of Reserve Money (RM) gets multiplied. If people keep some cash (c) and banks hold some in reserve (r), then not all money gets re-deposited. The multiplier α tells you how many rupees of M3 arise from each rupee of RM.
WORKED EXAMPLE Q.2 from paper
Given: Public keeps Rs10 cash, Rs50 in deposits. Money multiplier = 5. Find CRR.
1

c = Cash / Deposits = 10/50 = 0.2

2

α = (1+c)/(c+r) → 5 = (1+0.2)/(0.2+r)

3

5(0.2+r) = 1.2 → 1 + 5r = 1.2 → 5r = 0.2 → r = 0.04 = 4%

4

But CRR is as % of bank book (deposits) = 4/50 × 100 ... Actually r IS the CRR ratio = 0.04. As percent of deposits, it's already 4% ≈ answer B. 5% is closest but exact = 4% → D. None of the above

✓ ANSWERD — None of the above (CRR = 4%)

Reserve Money (RM) & Its Sources

Reserve Money (also called "base money" or "high-powered money") is the foundation of the money supply. It's the money the RBI directly controls.

Formula 3 — Change in Reserve Money
ΔRM = RBI OMO purchases
+ RBI repo operations
+ Δ RBI's credit to Government
+ Δ RBI's credit to banks/commercial sector
+ Δ Net Foreign Assets (NFA) / FX purchases
SourceEffect on RMExample
OMO Purchase↑ Increases RMRBI buys G-secs from banks → gives cash
OMO Sale↓ Decreases RMRBI sells G-secs → takes cash away
FX Purchase↑ Increases RMRBI buys USD → injects Rs
FX Sale↓ Decreases RMRBI sells USD → absorbs Rs
Repo↑ TemporarilyBanks borrow from RBI
Reverse Repo↓ TemporarilyBanks park money with RBI
WORKED EXAMPLE Q.3 from paper
RBI needs to infuse RM of Rs50. It has already done OMO purchases of Rs30. How much FX must it buy?
1

ΔRM = OMO + FX purchases

2

50 = 30 + FX purchases

3

FX purchases = 50 − 30 = Rs20

✓ ANSWERA — Rs20

Liquidity: Autonomous & Discretionary

Liquidity in the banking system determines whether banks have surplus or deficit funds — and this directly drives short-term interest rates.

Formula 5 — Total Liquidity
Total Liquidity = Autonomous Liquidity (AL) + Discretionary Liquidity (DL)
Formula 6 — Autonomous Liquidity (AL)
AL = Credit to Government
+ RBI's FX purchases
− Currency (in circulation)
− Bank Reserves
Why "Autonomous"? These flows happen automatically due to government fiscal operations, FX intervention, and how much cash the public holds — not due to any explicit RBI decision on rates.
Formula 8 — Discretionary Liquidity (DL)
DL = RBI OMO purchases + RBI Repo operations
(Note: reverse repos are negative — they absorb liquidity)
WORKED EXAMPLE Q.4 — AL
Credit to govt = Rs10, RBI FX sales = Rs50, Currency = Rs40, Bank reserves = Rs4
1

AL = Credit to Govt + FX purchases − Currency − Bank Reserves

2

Careful: FX sales = −Rs50 (sales drain liquidity, purchases inject)

3

AL = 10 + (−50) − 40 − 4 = 10 − 50 − 40 − 4 = −Rs84

✓ ANSWERC — Rs −84
WORKED EXAMPLE Q.5 — DL
OMO purchases = Rs50, Reverse repos = Rs50
1

DL = OMO + Repo − Reverse Repo

2

= 50 + 0 − 50 = Rs0

✓ ANSWERB — Rs0
WORKED EXAMPLE Q.6 — Total Liquidity
AL = −84 (from Q4), DL = 0 (from Q5)
1

Total = AL + DL = −84 + 0 = −Rs84

✓ ANSWERD — None of the above (−Rs84)

Call Rates, Excess Reserves & OIS

Formula 9/10 — Excess Reserves & Call Rates
Excess Reserves = Credit to Govt + FX purchases − Currency − CRR + DL

If Excess Reserves > 0: Banks park surplus → Call Rate = Reverse Repo Rate
If Excess Reserves < 0: Banks borrow from RBI → Call Rate = Repo Rate
The corridor: The RBI operates a corridor between the reverse repo rate (floor) and repo rate (ceiling). Banks with surplus park at the reverse repo; banks in deficit borrow at the repo. The call rate bounces between these two rates depending on system liquidity.
WORKED EXAMPLE Q.7 from paper
Excess Reserves = deficit of Rs100. RBI repo = 6.25%, reverse repo = 6%.
1

Deficit = Excess Reserves < 0

2

Banks must borrow from RBI at the repo rate

3

Call rate = 6.25%

✓ ANSWERA — 6.25%

OIS — Overnight Index Swap

What is OIS? An OIS is an interest rate swap where one party pays a fixed rate and the other pays a floating rate tied to the overnight call rate (like the RBI repo rate).

• Party that pays fixed = Investor 1
• Party that pays floating = Investor 2

If actual call rate > fixed rate: Investor 2 (floating payer) pays more → Investor 1 profits
If actual call rate < fixed rate: Investor 1 (fixed payer) overpays → Investor 1 loses
WORKED EXAMPLE Q.8 from paper
OIS trade: check the table from your paper. Key principle — if the realized floating rate > fixed rate paid by Investor 1, they profit. Calculate the net difference on notional principal.
1

Identify fixed rate paid by Investor 1 and average realized floating rate

2

If realized rate − fixed rate = +1.5% → Investor 1 profits 1.5%

3

If realized rate − fixed rate = −1.5% → Investor 1 loses 1.5%

PRINCIPLECompare fixed vs realized floating rate

Credit Market & Excess Bank Reserves

Formula 12 — Excess Bank Reserves
Excess Bank Reserves =
Deposits + Other Liabilities + RBI Borrowing
− CRR − Credit Demand − SLR − Foreign Currency Assets
Logic: A bank has sources of funds (deposits, liabilities, RBI borrowings) and uses of funds (CRR — mandatory cash reserve, SLR — mandatory govt securities, actual credit extended, FX assets). Whatever's left = excess reserves that can buy more G-secs or be deployed elsewhere.
ItemSource (+) or Use (−)
Deposits+ Source
Other Liabilities+ Source
RBI Borrowing+ Source
CRR (Cash Reserve Ratio)− Mandatory use (4% of deposits typically)
SLR (Statutory Liquidity Ratio)− Mandatory G-sec holding (20%)
Credit Demand− Loans given out
Foreign Currency Assets− FX holdings
WORKED EXAMPLE Q.9 from paper
Deposits = Rs100, CRR = Rs4, Credit demand = Rs60, SLR = Rs20, FX assets = Rs10
1

Excess Reserves = Deposits − CRR − Credit − SLR − FX

2

= 100 − 4 − 60 − 20 − 10 = Rs6

✓ ANSWERA — Rs6

G-Sec Market: Supply, Demand & RBI OMO

The Government Securities (G-Sec) market is where the government borrows. Understanding the supply-demand dynamics is crucial.

Supply & Demand Framework
Supply = Centre Borrowings (Net) + State Borrowings

Demand = Bank Demand + FPIs + Non-Banks + RBI OMO

Bank G-sec Demand = Deposits + Other Liabilities + RBI Borrowing
− Bank Reserves − Credit Demand − FX Assets
RBI OMO Formula
RBI OMO = ΔRM − RBI Repos − ΔRBI credit to banks
− Δ Net Foreign Assets − ΔRBI NNML

Bond Pricing & Yield to Maturity

Formula 24 — Fair Price of a Bond
Fair Price = Σ [Coupon / (1+r)ᵗ] + [Face Value / (1+r)ⁿ]

Where r = discount rate/YTM, t = period, n = maturity
Principle: A bond is worth the present discounted value of all its future cash flows — coupons paid periodically + face value at maturity. Discount at the market yield.
WORKED EXAMPLE Q.10 — 2-year bond
Example from class: a 2-year bond with given coupon and YTM. Apply the formula.
1

Price = C/(1+r)¹ + C/(1+r)² + FV/(1+r)²

2

E.g. if Coupon=100, FV=1000, r=5%: = 100/1.05 + 100/1.1025 + 1000/1.1025

3

= 95.24 + 90.70 + 907.03 = Rs1092.97

PRINCIPLEDiscount all future cash flows at YTM

Types of Yield

Three Yield Measures
Coupon Yield = Coupon / Face Value

Current Yield = Coupon / Purchase Price

YTM (approx) = [Annual Coupon + (FV − PV)/n] / [(FV + PV)/2]
WORKED EXAMPLE — YTM
Bond: Face Value = Rs100, Coupon = Rs7, Purchase Price = Rs104, Maturity = 2 years
1

YTM = [7 + (100 − 104)/2] / [(100 + 104)/2]

2

= [7 + (−2)] / [102]

3

= 5 / 102 = 4.9% ≈ 5%

✓ FROM LECTUREYTM ≈ 5% when P = 104
Key Relationship: Price ↑ ↔ Yield ↓. When a bond trades above face value (premium), YTM < coupon rate. When below face value (discount), YTM > coupon rate.

Duration & Modified Duration

Duration measures a bond's price sensitivity to interest rate changes — how much the price will move for a 1% move in yield.

Formula 28 — Macaulay Duration
Duration = Σ [t × PV(CFₜ)] / Price

= weighted average time to receive cash flows, weighted by PV
Formula 29 — Modified Duration
Modified Duration = Macaulay Duration / (1 + YTM)

%ΔPrice ≈ −Modified Duration × ΔYield
WORKED EXAMPLE Q.14 & Q.15 from paper
From the table in your exam. Duration is calculated by weighting each year's cash flow PV by time.
1

Calculate PV of each cash flow: CF₁/(1+r)¹, CF₂/(1+r)² etc.

2

Duration = (1×PV₁ + 2×PV₂ + ... + n×PVₙ) / Total Price

3

If Duration = 2.5 (Q14 answer A), then:

4

Modified Duration = 2.5 / (1 + YTM). If YTM ≈ 0.136: ModDur = 2.5/1.136 ≈ 2.2

✓ ANSWERQ14: A (2.5) | Q15: A (2.2)
🧠 DURATION INTUITION

A bond with longer duration is MORE sensitive to rate changes. Zero-coupon bonds have duration = maturity (all cash flow at end). Coupon bonds have duration < maturity (some cash flows come earlier). Modified Duration of 5 means: if yields rise by 1%, bond price drops by ~5%.

Nostro Balances (Q.18)

Nostro account = "Our account held with you" — an Indian bank's account held at a foreign bank in foreign currency. Drawdown = positive (using up the balance). Track inflows (receipts from exports, etc.) vs outflows (import payments). Net Nostro change = Inflows − Outflows.

ConceptDefinitionExample
Original MaturityTerm at the time of issue5-year loan issued 4 years ago → original = 5 yr

NEER & REER

Formula 37 — NEER (Nominal Effective Exchange Rate)
NEER = Geometric weighted mean of bilateral exchange rates

NEER = Π (eᵢ)^wᵢ
where eᵢ = bilateral rate with partner i, wᵢ = trade weight
Formula 38 — REER (Real Effective Exchange Rate)
REER = NEER × (P_domestic / P_foreign) adjusted

REER = Π (eᵢ × P / Pᵢ)^wᵢ
Adjusts NEER for relative inflation between countries
NEER vs REER: NEER only looks at nominal exchange rates. REER adjusts for price differences. If India has higher inflation than its trading partners, the rupee is "really" more expensive even if the nominal rate hasn't changed — REER captures this. REER > 100 = currency overvalued (uncompetitive).
WORKED EXAMPLE Q.21 — NEER, Q.22 — REER
Table from paper: bilateral rates and trade weights for countries B and C
1

NEER = (rate_B)^(wB) × (rate_C)^(wC)

2

Use the weights and bilateral rates from the table

3

REER = NEER × (P_A/P_B)^wB × (P_A/P_C)^wC

4

Adjust using inflation rates: India=2%, B=4%, C=1%

PRINCIPLENEER ≈ 3 (A), REER ≈ 7 (A) — from paper

Interest Rate Parity & Import Cover

Formula 39 — Uncovered IRP (UIRP)
Sₜ = S₀ × (1 + i_B) / (1 + i_C)

Sₜ = Expected spot rate at time T
S₀ = Current spot rate
i_B = Interest rate in country B (base)
i_C = Interest rate in country C (quote)
Formula 40 — Covered IRP (CIRP)
F₀ = S₀ × (1 + i_B) / (1 + i_C)

F₀ = Forward rate (locked in via forward contract)
Same formula — difference is: UIRP is expectation, CIRP is locked forward
Intuition: Higher interest rates attract capital, which appreciates the currency. So if Country B has higher rates, its currency appreciates — meaning you get fewer units of B per unit of C. The formulas capture this ratio of interest factors.
WORKED EXAMPLE Q.23 from paper
S₀ = 20, i_B = 10%, i_C = 5%
1

2

= 20 × 1.0476 = 20.95 ≈ 21

3

Answer C = 12.3 is wrong. Answer A = 19 is wrong. → D. None of the above

✓ ANSWERD — None of the above (≈20.95)
WORKED EXAMPLE Q.24 from paper
S₀ = 10, i_B = 10%, i_C = 10%
1

F₀ = 10 × (1.10) / (1.10) = 10 × 1 = 10

2

When rates are equal, no change in exchange rate

✓ ANSWERD — None of the above (F₀ = 10)

Import Cover (Q.25)

Formula 41 — Import Cover
Import Cover = FX Reserves / (Monthly Imports)

Monthly Imports = Annual Imports / 12
Note: Use TOTAL IMPORTS, not net of exports
WORKED EXAMPLE Q.25 from paper
Exports=200, Imports=240, Invisibles=30, FX Reserves=100
1

Monthly imports = 240/12 = Rs20 per month

2

Import Cover = 100 / 20 = 5 months

✓ ANSWERC — 5 months
Remember: Import cover uses GROSS imports (not net of exports). Invisibles are NOT subtracted from imports for this calculation. India typically targets ~6–9 months of import cover.
